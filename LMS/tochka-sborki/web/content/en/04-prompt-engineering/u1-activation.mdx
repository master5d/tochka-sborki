<Phase type="activation">

Picture a prompt you once fired at AI — and got back something useless.

Now don't think of it as a chat message. Picture it as **ordering a bespoke suit from a tailor while mumbling under your breath**: "something dark… roughly my size…" The tailor nods politely and cuts blind.

Run it in your head: what kind of "suit" came back? What went unsaid — what did the AI have to guess?

</Phase>

<Phase type="reflection">

Now picture a **detective arriving at the scene** and examining that prompt as evidence.

Hold the image: an empty room, no labels, no trail of intent. What clues are missing? What does the absence of a role, a criterion, a boundary tell an experienced investigator about the person who left it?

Notice what the scene reveals — and catch what that means for every prompt you reach for next time.

</Phase>

<Phase type="concept">

## Why bad prompts give bad results

An LLM is a prediction machine. It continues your text the way a typical person would in that context.

When you write `"Write an article about marketing"` — the model guesses:
- For whom? (some random audience)
- How long? (standard length)
- What style? (neutral)
- What focus? (general)

**Formula for a bad prompt:**
```
Task without context = assumptions without criteria = lottery
```

**Formula for a good prompt:**
```
Role + Task + Inputs + Expected output + Constraints = reproducible result
```

| Bad prompt | Good prompt |
|---------------|----------------|
| 2–3 words | 5–15 sentences |
| 5–7 iterations | 1–2 iterations |
| 60% hit rate | 95% hit rate |
| Different every time | Reproducible |

</Phase>
